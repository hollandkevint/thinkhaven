# ICP Definition + Go-to-Market Playbook

## What We're Building

A go-to-market playbook built around two ideal customer profiles, organized as journey maps from discovery through paid conversion. Each ICP gets a persona card, a simulated ThinkHaven session walkthrough, and a mapped content/distribution strategy.

**Goal:** Know exactly what to produce, where to post it, and how to collect feedback, so Kevin can move from pre-distribution to first real users.

## Why This Approach

ThinkHaven has strong product fundamentals (board of directors, lean canvas, progressive session loop) but zero distribution yet. The existing marketing content (MARKETING-LAUNCH-CONTENT.md) targets broad categories ("PMs," "founders," "strategic thinkers") without sharp enough personas to guide what Kevin writes and where he posts.

Journey-first because the highest-leverage output isn't "who are they" in the abstract. It's "what LinkedIn post makes ICP 1 click, what /try experience converts them, and what makes them pay $39."

## ICP 1: The Multi-Bet Operator

### Persona Card

**Name:** "Alex" (composite)
**Title:** Fractional CPO / Serial builder / Portfolio solopreneur
**Demographics:** 32-45, 8-15 years experience, earning $150-300K across 2-4 engagements
**Situation:** Running 2-4 active bets simultaneously. One fractional role, one side project approaching a go/no-go decision, possibly an angel investment evaluation. Time is the scarcest resource.

**Pain points:**
- Makes 3-5 "should I keep going or kill this?" decisions per quarter
- Uses ChatGPT/Claude for brainstorming but gets agreeable, unstructured output
- Doesn't have a real board or co-founder to pressure-test with
- Decision quality degrades under time pressure. Defaults to gut when they should use frameworks
- Each bad "keep going" decision costs 2-3 months of wasted focus

**Current tools:** Claude/ChatGPT (daily), Notion for notes, maybe a mastermind group (monthly, too slow for real-time decisions)

**What they're NOT looking for:** Another AI chatbot. More productivity tools. Generic frameworks they already know.

**Buying trigger:** The moment they're staring at a decision and realize they're about to guess. "Should I commit to this client project?" "Is this side project worth another month?" "Should I invest in this founder?"

**Success metric:** "I made a better go/no-go decision in 20 minutes than I would have in a week of mulling."

### Journey Map: Discovery to Paid

#### Stage 1: Discovery (LinkedIn)

**Trigger:** Scrolling LinkedIn, sees a post from Kevin that names their exact situation.

**Content that hooks Alex:**
> "Running multiple bets? Here's the decision most multi-project operators get wrong: they evaluate each bet in isolation instead of against their portfolio. ThinkHaven runs a 15-minute pressure test with six AI advisors who ask the questions your co-founder would, if you had one."

**What makes them click:** Specificity. "Multiple bets" and "portfolio" are words they use internally. The promise of 15 minutes maps to their time constraint. "Questions your co-founder would ask" names the gap.

**CTA:** "Try a free session. No signup." -> links to /try (guest limit is 10 messages per CLAUDE.md, landing page says 5 -- reconcile before launch)

#### Stage 2: First Touch (/try)

**Alex types:** "I'm evaluating whether to take on a new fractional CPO role at a Series A health tech company. I already have two other clients and a side project."

**Mary responds:** "That's a portfolio allocation question, not just a client fit question. Let me challenge this from the portfolio angle first. What happens to your two existing clients and the side project if you say yes? Be specific about hours."

**Alex's reaction:** "Oh. She didn't just say 'here are the pros and cons.' She reframed the question." This is the moment of differentiation from ChatGPT.

**By message 3:** Mary has surfaced that the side project is the real decision. The new client role is a distraction from the harder go/no-go on the side project.

**By message 5 (guest limit):** Alex has a partial lean canvas for the side project and a clear "the fractional role is a safety blanket" insight. Signup prompt appears.

**Conversion blocker:** Alex needs to see that the board members add something Mary alone doesn't. The guest experience must tease the board. Mary should say: "I want to bring in Victoria to challenge the unit economics here, and Casey to ask whether you actually want to spend your weekends on this. Sign up to continue with the full board."

#### Stage 3: Signup + First Full Session

**What Alex does:** Signs up (Google OAuth, 10 seconds). Starts a new session. Picks up where /try left off or starts fresh.

**Board engagement:**
- **Victoria:** "You said this side project could be a $2M ARR business. Walk me through how. Who pays? What's the buying motion? Because right now I'm hearing a hobby with revenue fantasies."
- **Casey:** "Real talk. You've got two paying clients and a side project. If the side project fails, you're fine. If a client fires you because you're distracted, you're not. Which scenario scares you more?"
- **Omar:** "What ships in 90 days? Not what could ship. What will you actually build, with what time, given your other commitments?"

**Session output:** Lean canvas with honest assessments. The "Unfair Advantage" box is empty, which tells Alex something important.

**Alex's reaction:** "This was more useful than the last three mastermind calls combined. And it took 18 minutes."

#### Stage 4: Conversion to Paid ($39)

**Trigger:** Alex burns through 5 free sessions in 2 weeks (one per active bet). Hits the paywall.

**Decision calculus:** $39 for 10 sessions = $3.90/decision. Alex bills clients $200-400/hr. A single bad decision costs 40+ hours. The ROI math is trivial.

**What would PREVENT conversion:** If the sessions felt repetitive. If Mary asked the same questions every time. If the lean canvas felt like a template exercise rather than genuine pressure-testing.

## ICP 2: The Strategy Practitioner

### Persona Card

**Name:** "Jordan" (composite)
**Title:** Independent strategy consultant / Fractional Head of Strategy / Agency strategist
**Demographics:** 35-50, 10-20 years experience, $200-500K revenue (solo or small team)
**Situation:** Advises 3-8 clients per year on product strategy, market entry, or business model decisions. Constantly looking for tools that make them faster and more structured. Deliverables are their product.

**Pain points:**
- Client engagements start with messy, unstructured "here's my idea" conversations
- Spends hours facilitating workshops that could be pre-structured
- Needs to rapidly get to a structured artifact (lean canvas, decision brief) to justify their rate
- Worries about AI replacing them. Wants to use AI to amplify, not get replaced by it
- Solo practitioners don't have a team to bounce ideas off before presenting to clients

**Current tools:** Miro/FigJam for workshops, ChatGPT for research, custom frameworks in Notion/Google Docs

**Two usage angles:**

**Angle A: For their own practice**
Jordan uses ThinkHaven to pressure-test their OWN strategic recommendations before presenting to clients. "Let me run my proposed strategy through the board and see if Victoria finds holes in the economics before the client does."

**Angle B: As a client-facing tool**
Jordan runs ThinkHaven sessions WITH clients (or assigns them as pre-work). "Before our next strategy session, run your idea through ThinkHaven and bring the lean canvas. We'll start from a structured foundation instead of a blank whiteboard."

**Buying trigger (Angle A):** About to present a strategy recommendation to a client and wants to stress-test it first.
**Buying trigger (Angle B):** Tired of spending the first 2 hours of every engagement getting the client to articulate their actual problem.

**Success metric (A):** "The board found a gap in my recommendation that would have embarrassed me in front of the client."
**Success metric (B):** "My client came to our session with a filled lean canvas. We skipped 2 hours of discovery and went straight to strategy."

### Journey Map: Discovery to Paid

#### Stage 1: Discovery (LinkedIn or Slack)

**Trigger (LinkedIn):** Kevin posts about structured decision-making frameworks. Jordan, who lives and breathes frameworks, engages.

**Content that hooks Jordan:**
> "I built an AI tool that runs the facilitation process I used to do manually: challenge assumptions, pressure-test from investor/operator/co-founder angles, and output a lean canvas in 15 minutes. Not a chatbot. A structured session with six distinct advisor perspectives."

**What makes them click:** "Facilitation process" and "structured session" are Jordan's language. The multi-perspective angle maps to what they do manually in workshops. They're curious whether this could augment their practice.

**Trigger (Slack - HealthTechNerds/Lenny's):**
> "Anyone tried AI tools for pre-session client prep? I built ThinkHaven to run structured strategy sessions that output lean canvases. Looking for consultants/strategists to test it and tell me if this would fit into your workflow."

**What makes them click:** "Pre-session client prep" names a real workflow pain point. "Fit into your workflow" signals this isn't trying to replace them.

#### Stage 2: First Touch (/try)

**Jordan types:** "I'm advising a B2B SaaS startup on whether to expand into the mid-market segment. They're currently enterprise-only with 12 customers."

**Mary responds:** "Before we evaluate the mid-market move, I want to understand what's driving this. Is the founder seeing enterprise sales cycles that are too long? Hitting a TAM ceiling? Or getting inbound from smaller companies they're turning away?"

**Jordan's reaction:** "She asked the diagnostic question I would have asked. Good."

**By message 3:** Mary has challenged whether "mid-market" is the right frame. Maybe the real question is "should we add a self-serve tier?" which is a different GTM motion entirely.

**By message 5:** Jordan has a partial lean canvas reframing the question. They're thinking: "This would be incredible pre-work for my client."

**Conversion blocker for Jordan:** They need to see the EXPORT quality. If the lean canvas output is professional enough to send to a client or drop into a strategy deck, they'll pay. If it looks like ChatGPT output, they won't.

#### Stage 3: Signup + Exploring Both Angles

**Angle A session:** Jordan runs their own recommendation through the board before a client meeting.
- Victoria challenges the revenue model: "Your client says $500K ARR in mid-market year one. Based on what pipeline? What conversion rate? Show me the math."
- Jordan realizes their client's projection has no bottoms-up basis. They restructure their recommendation.

**Angle B experiment:** Jordan tells a client: "Before our next call, go to thinkhaven.co/try and run your expansion idea through it. Bring the lean canvas."
- Client shows up with a structured artifact. Session starts at strategy, not discovery.
- Jordan saves 2 hours of facilitation time. That's $400-800 of value.

#### Stage 4: Conversion to Paid ($39)

**Decision calculus (Angle A):** $39 = insurance against presenting a weak recommendation. One saved embarrassment justifies 10x the cost.

**Decision calculus (Angle B):** If Jordan recommends ThinkHaven to 3-4 clients as pre-work, they'd want their own Pro account to preview sessions and refine their approach. $39 is invisible against their engagement fees.

**Expansion opportunity:** Jordan might want to buy sessions for clients, or want a "consultant" tier that lets them manage client sessions. This is a future pricing conversation, not a v1 feature.

## Simulated Session Walkthrough: Alex (Multi-Bet Operator)

### Setup
Alex has three active bets: (1) fractional CPO at a health tech startup, (2) a side project building a niche SaaS for veterinary clinics, and (3) evaluating an angel investment in a friend's company. Alex opens /try.

### The Session

**Alex:** "I need to decide whether to invest $25K in my friend's AI tutoring startup. I've got two other things going on and my bandwidth is limited."

**Mary:** "Before we talk about the investment, I want to understand the portfolio. You mentioned two other things. What are they, and how much of your weekly bandwidth does each consume?"

**Alex:** "Fractional CPO role takes about 25 hours/week. My vet clinic SaaS side project is maybe 10 hours/week. The angel investment would mostly be money, not time, but my friend wants me as an advisor too."

**Mary:** "So you're at 35 hours/week on active commitments, and your friend is asking for capital plus advisory time on top. Let's challenge the 'mostly money, not time' assumption. In your experience with advisory roles, what does the actual time commitment look like after month one?"

**Alex:** "...Probably 3-5 hours a week once things get real. More during fundraising."

**Mary:** "So we're looking at 38-40 hours/week across three commitments, with surge risk during your friend's fundraise. Here's what I'm hearing as the real question: this isn't 'should I invest $25K?' It's 'which of my three bets gets cut when bandwidth hits zero?' I want to bring in Victoria to pressure-test the financial side and Casey to challenge whether you actually want a third commitment. [Sign up to continue with the full board]"

**[Alex signs up]**

**Victoria:** "Walk me through the $25K. What's the valuation? What ownership does that get you? And more importantly, what's your expected return timeline? Because $25K in an early-stage AI tutoring startup is a 7-10 year lockup with a high probability of zero."

**Alex:** "It's at a $2M pre-money valuation, so I'd get about 1.2%. Expected Series A in 18 months."

**Victoria:** "1.2% at $2M pre. Even if they 10x to a $20M Series A, your stake is worth $240K on paper. Not liquid. Meanwhile, your fractional CPO role pays what, $15-20K/month? The $25K angel check is one month of fractional income locked up for a decade. The REAL cost is the advisory hours. At your fractional rate, 4 hours/week for a year is $40-60K of opportunity cost. This is a $75K decision, not a $25K decision."

**Casey:** "Real talk. Is this about the investment math, or about not wanting to say no to your friend? Because those are completely different decisions."

**Alex:** "...Both, honestly."

**Casey:** "Then let's separate them. If this were a stranger's company with the same pitch, would you invest?"

**[Canvas updates: Problem box fills with "Portfolio over-allocation risk." Unfair Advantage stays empty for the tutoring startup. Key Metrics box fills with "Opportunity cost: $75K including advisory time."]**

**Omar:** "Here's what I want to know. Your vet clinic SaaS, how far along is it? Because from what I'm hearing, you have one paying commitment (fractional role), one bet that could become a real company (vet SaaS), and a proposed third bet that dilutes both. What ships on the vet SaaS in 90 days if you stay focused?"

### Session Output

**Lean Canvas (exported as markdown):**

| Box | Content |
|-----|---------|
| Problem | Portfolio over-allocation: 3 commitments at 40hr/week with surge risk. The angel investment masks an advisory time commitment worth $40-60K/year. |
| Customer Segments | N/A (personal decision, not a product) |
| Unique Value Prop | The real question isn't "invest or not" but "which bet gets priority when bandwidth hits zero?" |
| Solution | Option A: Decline investment, double down on vet SaaS. Option B: Invest but cap advisory at 2hr/month (not /week). Option C: Wind down vet SaaS, free bandwidth for both fractional + advisory. |
| Key Metrics | Opportunity cost of advisory: $40-60K/year. Vet SaaS 90-day milestone: [empty, needs defining]. Fractional role renewal date: [ask]. |
| Unfair Advantage | [Empty for the tutoring startup. Alex has no edge here.] |

**Alex's takeaway:** "I went in thinking this was a $25K financial decision. It's actually a portfolio allocation decision, and the board made me see that the vet SaaS is the bet I'm avoiding by staying busy with safer commitments."

## Simulated Session Walkthrough: Jordan (Strategy Practitioner)

### Setup
Jordan is preparing for a strategy engagement with a B2B SaaS client who wants to move downmarket from enterprise to mid-market.

### The Session

**Jordan:** "My client is an enterprise B2B SaaS company with 12 customers at $80-120K ACV. They want to add a mid-market tier at $15-25K ACV. I'm preparing my recommendation and want to pressure-test it."

**Mary:** "What's driving the mid-market push? Is the founder seeing something specific, or is this a 'we need more logos' instinct?"

**Jordan:** "A bit of both. Enterprise sales cycles are 6-9 months. The founder wants faster revenue and thinks mid-market is 60-90 day cycles."

**Mary:** "Two assumptions I want to challenge. First: that mid-market sales cycles will actually be faster. Second: that the product can serve both segments without becoming two products. Let me ask the harder question first. Is the founder trying to solve a SALES problem (long cycles) or a PRODUCT problem (wrong segment)? Because mid-market is a different answer for each."

**Jordan:** "That's a good reframe. It's probably a sales problem. They have product-market fit in enterprise."

**Mary:** "If it's a sales problem, mid-market might actually make it worse. You'd be splitting your 8-person sales team across two motions with different buyer personas, deal sizes, and onboarding requirements. I want to bring in Victoria on the economics and Omar on the operational reality."

**Victoria:** "12 enterprise customers at $100K average is $1.2M ARR. To match that in mid-market at $20K ACV, you need 60 customers. With a 10% close rate on qualified pipeline, that's 600 qualified leads per year. Does your client have that demand gen engine? Because enterprise and mid-market marketing are completely different channels."

**Omar:** "Here's what I'd want to know before recommending mid-market. How many support tickets per enterprise customer per month? Because mid-market means 5x the customers for the same revenue. If support scales linearly, you need 5x the customer success team. If your product isn't self-serve-ready, mid-market will crush your margins."

**[Canvas fills with competitive analysis in Problem box, customer segment differences, and a Key Metrics section showing the 12 vs. 60 customer math]**

**Jordan's takeaway:** "Victoria's demand gen question is the one my client hasn't answered. I'm going to restructure my recommendation: before going mid-market, prove you can generate 50 qualified mid-market leads/month. If you can't, the sales cycle problem is better solved by improving enterprise pipeline velocity."

## Distribution + Feedback Strategy

### How to Share

**LinkedIn (primary):**
- Post 2x/week, alternating between ICP-specific hooks and "building in public" updates
- Every post ends with /try CTA (no signup required)
- Engage in comments. Every comment is a potential DM conversion.

**Slack (secondary):**
- One post per community. Not spammy. Frame as "looking for feedback," not "buy my thing."
- Follow up in-thread with anyone who responds.

**Direct DMs (highest conversion):**
- After every LinkedIn post, DM 5-10 people who engage
- Use the DM template from MARKETING-LAUNCH-CONTENT.md, adapted per ICP
- For Alex-types: "You're running multiple things. This tool pressure-tests whether each one is worth your time."
- For Jordan-types: "You advise on strategy. Would you use a tool that pre-structures client conversations?"

### How to Collect Feedback

**In-app (automated):**
- FeedbackModal triggers after session 1 and session 3
- Track: feedback_type (praise/bug/feature_request), free_text, session count at time of feedback

**Post-session DM (manual, high-signal):**
- After someone completes a session, Kevin DMs them personally
- Three questions: (1) Was this more useful than ChatGPT for this decision? (2) What was the single most valuable moment? (3) Would you pay $4/session for this?
- Goal: 10 of these conversations in the first 2 weeks

**LinkedIn comments (public signal):**
- Watch for patterns: what language do people use to describe their situation?
- Feed back into post copy for the next cycle

## Key Decisions

1. **Two ICPs to start:** Multi-Bet Operator (Alex) and Strategy Practitioner (Jordan). Expand to 4-5 only after validating with real users.
2. **Journey-first approach:** Content and distribution mapped to the specific funnel stage, not generic "awareness" posts.
3. **LinkedIn + Slack communities as primary channels.** No paid, no email sequences yet. Manual, high-touch.
4. **Guest experience (/try) is the conversion engine.** Every CTA points there. No signup friction.
5. **Board tease in guest experience is critical.** Mary must explicitly offer the board by message 4-5 to differentiate from ChatGPT.
6. **Feedback is manual + in-app.** DM conversations for depth, FeedbackModal for volume.

## Decision Needed: Lead ICP for Week 1

Pick ONE ICP to concentrate all week 1 distribution on. Two posts targeting different personas in the same week splits signal when you have zero baseline data.

**Case for Alex first:** Kevin's network has more multi-hat operators and fractional execs. HealthTechNerds is home turf. The "running multiple bets" hook is more visceral and less crowded than "strategy tool for consultants."

**Case for Jordan first:** The consultant angle has a built-in multiplier (Jordan sends clients to ThinkHaven). Faster feedback loop because Jordan evaluates tools professionally. Export quality feedback comes immediately.

**Recommendation:** Alex first. Kevin has direct network access to this persona, and the pain point ("I'm about to guess on a big decision") is more urgent than "I need better client pre-work." Use week 1 results to decide whether week 2 adds Jordan or doubles down on Alex.

## Risk: Simulated Sessions Show Best-Case Only

Both session walkthroughs above show ThinkHaven performing at its peak. Before real distribution, Kevin should run 2-3 sessions himself using the exact opening messages from the simulations and note where the product falls short:

- Does Mary actually reframe the question, or give a generic "let's explore the pros and cons"?
- Does the board add distinct value, or do Victoria/Casey/Omar sound alike?
- Is the lean canvas output specific enough to be useful, or does it read like a template?

These gaps become the product punch list before sharing with real users.
